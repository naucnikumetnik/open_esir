Yes. In Serbia, the compliance burden is basically: your ESIR and/or LPFR product has to be approved by the Tax Administration before customers can legally use that model. This is not some vague “be compliant” checkbox. The official process is run by the Poreska uprava itself, and the public register lists approved elements by type, model/version, supplier, decision number, and approval date.

The approval flow is roughly this. First, you register as a supplier in the Tax Administration’s supplier development environment / sandbox. In that registration you provide legal-entity or entrepreneur data and contact details; then you confirm registration by email; then PURS reviews it; and if approved, it issues the authentication certificates you need to access the supplier portal and development environment. The guide explicitly says the process starts with supplier registration in the supplier development environment, and the registration flow includes confirmation and issuance of certificates for portal access.

After that, you build and test your product and submit an electronic application for technical review. The supplier portal is supposed to give you detailed instructions, digital forms, the list of required documents, support, and access to LPFR testing tools. For each approval, the supplier must also submit a self-assessment checklist. The technical guide says the review has two parts: a technical review for functional compliance with the technical guide, and an administrative review to confirm the documentation is complete. The technical review is done by the Technical Team of the Tax Administration, not by some outside lab mentioned in the public guide.

The documentation burden is real. The guide says the application must include supporting documentation, and in general all necessary documents must be in Serbian. For ESIR, the self-assessment/documentation section includes a product description, user manual, and for transferable approval also installation and configuration instructions. For LPFR, the documentation includes a product description, a detailed user manual, and installation/integration instructions. There is one softer carve-out: for a non-transferable ESIR used only for your own business, the user manual may be in English with basic installation information.

There are also two approval modes, and this matters for your strategy. Transferable approval (prenosivo) is the one you need if you want to sell or supply the solution to customers. Non-transferable approval (neprenosivo) is for a fiscalization taxpayer who develops one or more EFU elements for their own use only. So if you want to build a competing product and sell it to restaurants, you are not looking for the easy own-use route; you need the transferable route.

Once the technical review is successfully completed, only then do you get the ability to submit the formal approval request, and from that moment the guide says the Tax Administration’s 15-day statutory deadline for issuing the decision starts running. If approved, the decision includes the approval report and the EFU element ID, and the product is published in the public register.

The nasty part is that this is not just a one-time hurdle. The guide says every new version that affects the appearance of the fiscal receipt requires a new approval, and PURS can revoke approval if the ESIR or LPFR is no longer compliant with the technical guide. The approved supplier is then expected to keep the product aligned, stop selling the affected model, and even withdraw affected solutions from the market if approval is revoked.

So the blunt answer is: getting “certified” is not some fast paperwork trick. It is a product approval workflow with sandbox registration, portal access, certificates, documentation, self-assessment, technical review, administrative review, and ongoing version-control discipline. The good news is that the barrier is real but not impossible — the public register shows many approved transferable ESIRs and LPFRs from Serbian vendors and some non-transferable ones too.

The approval process is survivable, but it is the wrong first moat for most founders.

Think of Serbia’s eFiscalization as a state-controlled fiscal core with private software/hardware plugged into it. The central state actor is Poreska uprava, which runs the SUF (Sistem za upravljanje fiskalizacijom), defined as the Tax Administration’s software system that receives data from fiscal receipt processors, manages them through their lifecycle, and produces analyses/reports. The thing the seller actually uses is an EFU (elektronski fiskalni uređaj), and the technical guide says an EFU consists of one PFR, one or more ESIRs, and a BE issued by Poreska uprava.

Here are the important terms in plain language:

ESIR = Elektronski sistem za izdavanje računa. This is the software/hardware element where the merchant enters sale data and from which the receipt is issued. In normal language: the cashier app, POS app, webshop fiscal frontend, or whatever UI the seller uses.
PFR = Procesor fiskalnih računa. This is the fiscal engine. The guide says it exchanges data with the ESIR and the security element, analyzes the transaction, formats it as a fiscal receipt, creates the electronic signature, stores fiscal data, and transmits the data to SUF.
L-PFR = Lokalni procesor fiskalnih računa. Same fiscal engine concept, but running locally at the place where receipts are issued.
V-PFR = Virtuelni procesor fiskalnih računa. The PFR that lives in the Tax Administration’s network rather than on the merchant’s local setup.
BE = Bezbednosni element. This is the certificate-bearing security element issued by Poreska uprava. It is used to authenticate the taxpayer and to sign receipts. PURS says it can be a smart card or a file, and that it is personalized to the taxpayer, business space, and the matching fiscal device.
ESF = Elektronski servis za fiskalizaciju. This is the electronic service merchants use, through ePorezi, for administrative actions like requesting a security element.
ePorezi = the general Tax Administration portal for electronic services. PURS describes it as the portal for electronically signed submissions, status tracking, account overview, and permissions management.

Now the architecture.

At the top sits Poreska uprava. It does not run the merchant’s restaurant app or bakery routing app. It runs the state fiscal infrastructure: SUF, approval workflows for EFU components, issuance of security elements, and public receipt verification. Customers and inspectors can verify receipts via the receipt-verification system, and the technical guide says fiscal receipts can be checked by scanning a QR code on printed receipts or opening a verification hyperlink on electronic receipts.

Below that sits the seller — the restaurant, bakery, store, pharmacy, whoever is the obveznik fiskalizacije. Before using an EFU, the taxpayer has to submit business-space data to Poreska uprava, and then an authorized person requests the BE through ESF via ePorezi. PURS also states that a receipt issued without using the security element is not considered a fiscal receipt under the law.

Below that sits the private fiscal device stack used by the seller. The seller chooses an EFU type. The technical guide says the taxpayer may choose either an EFU with its own/local PFR, which can issue receipts even during temporary or permanent internet outages, or an EFU that uses the PFR in SUF. That is the key split between L-PFR and V-PFR.

The basic flow of one sale is:

The merchant enters transaction data into the ESIR.
The ESIR sends transaction data to the PFR.
The PFR uses the BE to process, sign, and format the fiscal receipt.
The receipt is returned to the ESIR, which issues it to the buyer.
Fiscal data is transmitted to SUF, or retained and later transmitted in the local/offline case.
The customer can later verify the receipt through the QR code or verification link.

So where do you sit?

That depends on how much of the stack you want to own.

Case 1: You are only an app/integrator.
You build the restaurant delivery app, bakery route system, order board, POS wrapper, etc., but you do not own an approved ESIR or PFR. In that case, your software sits above the fiscal layer and hands fiscal actions off to someone else’s approved solution. You are basically a business-software vendor or integrator, not a fiscal-component supplier.

Case 2: You own the ESIR layer.
Here, your product becomes the approved receipt-issuing frontend used by the merchant. You own the cashier/webshop/POS UI that captures the sale and issues the receipt, but the PFR may still be someone else’s — either a local L-PFR or the virtual PFR path. In this case, you are no longer “just an integrator”; you are a supplier of EFU components. The technical guide defines the supplier as the person delivering the hardware/software solution needed for EFU operation.

Case 3: You own the ESIR and the L-PFR.
Then you are effectively building a full fiscal device stack, not just an app. That gives you maximum control and maximum burden: approval, testing, maintenance, and the change-control obligations tied to the approved model.

The official system even makes this distinction legally. The guide says transferable approval is for a supplier that wants to deliver the hardware/software solution to taxpayers, while non-transferable approval is for a taxpayer developing EFU elements for its own needs only. So if you want to sell this layer to other merchants, you are in the transferable/supplier position, not the own-use position.

So the shortest accurate mental model is:

Poreska uprava = regulator + central fiscal backend + approvals + security elements + verification.
Seller = taxpayer using the system in a registered business space.
Customer = gets receipt and can verify it.
You = either an app vendor on top of the fiscal layer, or a formal supplier of EFU components if you own the ESIR/PFR layer.

If you own only the automatable business layer, you are outside the strict fiscal core.
If you own the ESIR, you sit at the border between business workflow and the state fiscal core.
If you own ESIR + L-PFR, you are inside the fiscal core as a certified component supplier.

My blunt take: the smartest first position is usually Case 1 or Case 2, not Case 3. Owning the full PFR layer is where the extra pain starts.

Yes — in principle, you could open-source a generic ESIR and LPFR. I did not find an official PURS rule saying the source code must be closed or that an approved ESIR/LPFR cannot be published under an open-source license. What PURS regulates is something else: whether a given ESIR/LPFR model and version is approved, whether it matches the approved behavior, and whether the supplier fulfills the approval obligations.

The catch is the important part: an open-source repo is not the same thing as a legally usable fiscal product in Serbia. The technical guide says every ESIR and/or LPFR must pass PURS approval, and even an ESIR/LPFR that a taxpayer develops for its own needs must also go through approval. The guide also says a taxpayer may use only the ESIR/LPFR model that PURS approved.

So the honest answer is:

Open-source publication: probably yes.
Deploying that code in production for Serbian fiscalization without approval: no.

Where this becomes tricky is forks and community modifications. PURS approval is tied to a specific type / name / version / element ID / supplier / approval type, and that is visible in the public register. The approval report also binds the supplier to place on the market the identical product in the case of transferable approval, or to use the identical product for own needs in the non-transferable case.

That means a hypothetical open-source setup could work like this:

you publish the code,
but only one pinned build is the approved Serbian production build,
and random community forks are just forks, not legally deployable fiscal products in Serbia unless they go through their own approval path.

This is reinforced by the update rules. If a change alters functionality or the appearance of delivered receipts, it must be reported through the supplier portal, and the applicant must submit a new technical review with updated hardware/software version and go through approval again. PURS also checks whether the model in use matches the model that was approved.

So the open-source part is not the blocker. The blocker is change control. An open-source fiscal core could exist, but Serbian production use would still need a strict release discipline. “Anyone can patch anything and deploy it” is the exact opposite of what the approval model is designed for.

There is another practical point: the PFR uses the taxpayer’s security element (BE) to sign receipts, and the guide defines the BE as the hardware/software element containing the taxpayer’s certificate used by the PFR for signing receipts. So even if the code is open, live deployments still depend on taxpayer-specific fiscal credentials and approved operation, not just on having the source code.

Also, if approval is revoked, the supplier is expected to stop selling that model and withdraw from the market the devices/software solutions whose approval was revoked. That makes a loose “community-maintained fiscal distro” awkward in practice, because somebody still has to act as the accountable supplier for the approved model.

So my blunt conclusion is:

Yes, open-sourcing a generic ESIR/LPFR is probably possible.
No, open source does not remove the supplier/approval burden.
And no, a public GitHub repo would not magically make community builds production-legal in Serbia.

The model that actually makes sense would be:

open-source core code,
one official certified distribution,
strict tagged releases for Serbia,
paid support/compliance around that distribution,
and forks treated as non-certified unless someone gets their own approval.

That is much more realistic than “fully open community fiscal stack where anyone deploys any branch.”

I did not find an explicit official prohibition on open-sourcing the code itself. The constraint is the approved model/version regime, not a visible “code must be proprietary” rule.

ESIR je zvanično “elektronski sistem za izdavanje računa”, to jest element u koji obveznik unosi podatke o prometu i iz kog se izdaje fiskalni račun. Zbog toga ESIR u praksi nije samo “čisti generator” u uskom smislu, nego obično obuhvata i:

ulazni sloj za unos prometa,
validaciju poslovnih podataka,
sklapanje računa,
i izdavanje računa prema korisniku.

Zato bih rekao:

naš generator računa = jezgro ESIR-a,
ali ako kod nas postoji i UI / kontrolni softver kroz koji kasir ili konobar unosi stavke, onda je i taj deo vrlo verovatno deo ESIR sloja, a ne nešto sasvim odvojeno.

PFR je zvanično element koji:

razmenjuje podatke sa ESIR-om i bezbednosnim elementom,
analizira podatke o prometu,
formatira ih kao fiskalni račun,
stvara elektronski potpis,
vraća fiskalni račun ESIR-u,
čuva podatke o prometu,
i prenosi fiskalne podatke u Sistem za upravljanje fiskalizacijom.
To je praktično tačno ono što smo mi zvali procesor fiskalizacije.

Za adapter za bezbednosni pristup je bitna jedna stvar:
u našoj arhitekturi ima smisla da ga odvojimo kao poseban interni modul, ali u zvaničnoj terminologiji on nije poseban EFU element. Zvanično postoje:

ESIR
PFR
BE kao bezbednosni element koji izdaje država.
A PFR je taj koji koristi BE za potpisivanje. Dakle, naš adapter je više podkomponenta PFR-a nego zaseban regulatorni entitet.

Najčistija mapa je ovakva:

naša interna podela

kontrolni softver
generator računa
procesor fiskalizacije
adapter za bezbednosni pristup

zvanična podela

ESIR = kontrolni softver + generator računa
PFR = procesor fiskalizacije + adapter za bezbednosni pristup
BE = državni bezbednosni element koji PFR koristi.

I još jedna bitna nijansa: tehničko uputstvo eksplicitno predviđa i varijantu gde su ESIR i lokalni PFR spojeni u jedan proizvod. Znači, čak ni država ne insistira da to budu fizički ili softverski odvojene celine, nego da njihove funkcionalne odgovornosti budu ispoštovane.

Ako hoćeš najkraću verziju za dalje razmišljanje:

ESIR = “primi promet i izdaj račun”
PFR = “fiskalizuj, potpiši i pošalji”
BE adapter = interni deo PFR-a, ne posebna državna kategorija.

Ako želiš, sledeće mogu da ti prevedem ovo u baš tvoj komponentni model sa portovima i interfejsima, tipa IReceiptComposition, IFiscalization, ISecurityElementAccess, ITaxAuthoritySubmission.

Ok, mi pravimo open source, jako modularno.koja je bese razlika izmedju lokalnog pfr- i eksternog?

Proveravam zvanične definicije da to zategnem bez nagađanja. Razlika je suštinski “gde živi fiskalni procesor i ko ga hostuje”, ali hoću da ti dam tačno šta to znači za arhitekturu, offline rad i odgovornost.

Kod virtuelnog procesora fiskalnih računa tvoj ESIR ne koristi tvoj lokalni fiskalni procesor, nego se oslanja na procesor u infrastrukturi Poreske uprave. To znači da je PFR “napolju”, van tvog privatnog hostinga. U supplier dokumentaciji se to vidi kroz posebne resurse za rad sa V-PFR scenarijem, kao što je VSDC Request submitter, namenjen dobavljačima ESIR-a za integraciju sa tim putem.

Najpraktičnija razlika je internet. Poreska uprava kaže da uređaj koji koristi procesor u mreži Poreske uprave omogućava izdavanje fiskalnih računa isključivo kroz stalnu internet vezu u realnom vremenu. Zbog toga čak propisuje da obveznik koji se osloni na taj model mora da ima makar jedan uređaj sa sopstvenim lokalnim procesorom fiskalnih računa, koji može da izdaje račune i kod privremenog ili trajnog prekida interneta. To je verovatno najvažnija poslovna razlika između ova dva modela.

Znači, grubo:

lokalni PFR: više kontrole, više odgovornosti, offline otpornost, više posla oko razvoja i approval-a.
virtuelni / eksterni PFR: manje toga gradiš u svom stack-u, ali zavisiš od stalne veze i od državnog procesa u realnom vremenu.

U tvom open-source, modularnom modelu to znači sledeće:

ako želiš maksimalnu nezavisnost i pun proizvod, praviš ESIR + lokalni PFR.
ako želiš tanji start, možeš prvo praviti ESIR koji se kači na virtuelni PFR, pa tek kasnije dodati svoj lokalni PFR. Supplier resursi Poreske uprave su i podeljeni baš tako: za dobavljače ESIR-a postoje razvojni alati poput Razvojnog L-PFR-a i VSDC Request submitter-a, a za dobavljače L-PFR-a posebni alati poput SE Sign Invoice i SDC Analyzer-a.

Moj iskren savet:
za dugoročno ozbiljan proizvod, lokalni PFR je jača pozicija.
Za brži ulazak i manje inicijalnog tereta, ESIR + virtuelni PFR je lakši početak.

Ako hoćeš, sledeće mogu da ti nacrtam potpuno praktično: koje odgovornosti ostaju u ESIR-u kad koristiš virtuelni PFR, a koje prelaze u lokalni PFR kad ga poseduješ ti.

Da — tvoj opis je skoro tačan, ali bitna ispravka je sledeća: ESIR ne “prebacuje” automatski na V-PFR samo zato što internet postoji. Moraš da razlikuješ dva različita režima rada:
ESIR → L-PFR ili ESIR → V-PFR. Poreska uprava eksplicitno razlikuje lokalni procesor fiskalnih računa, koji je “na lokaciji izdavanja računa”, od virtuelnog procesora, koji je “u mreži Poreske uprave”. Takođe navodi da je uređaj koji koristi procesor u mreži Poreske uprave vezan za izdavanje računa kroz stalnu internet vezu u realnom vremenu, dok je lokalni procesor tu da omogući rad i kad internet padne.

Ako radiš u L-PFR režimu, tok je ovakav: konobar ili kasir kroz tvoju aplikaciju unese promet; to ulazi u tvoj ESIR, jer je ESIR po definiciji element “u koji obveznik unosi podatke o prometu i iz kojeg se izdaje fiskalni račun”. Zatim ESIR šalje zahtev tvom L-PFR-u. PFR po zvaničnoj definiciji razmenjuje podatke sa ESIR-om i bezbednosnim elementom, analizira promet, formatira podatke kao fiskalni račun, stvara elektronski potpis, vraća fiskalni račun ESIR-u, čuva podatke o prometu i prenosi fiskalne podatke u sistem Poreske uprave. Bezbednosni element sadrži elektronski sertifikat obveznika i PFR ga koristi za potpisivanje računa.

Dakle, za L-PFR najtačnije je:
UI / kontrolni softver → ESIR → L-PFR + bezbednosni element → ESIR → kupac / štampač, a paralelno ili naknadno L-PFR → SUF / Poreska uprava. Poreska uprava u FAQ-u kaže da L-PFR beleži i elektronski potpisuje transakcije do trenutka ponovnog uspostavljanja internet veze i da postoji obaveza čuvanja podataka u internoj memoriji do prenosa podataka Poreskoj upravi. Tehničko uputstvo dodatno kaže da L-PFR mora da skladišti podatke za iščitavanje lokalno pre nego što ESIR-u vrati odgovor sa fiskalizovanim podacima.

Znači, u L-PFR režimu internet ne menja glavni tok fiskalizacije. Ako internet postoji, L-PFR može odmah da šalje podatke ka SUF-u. Ako internet ne postoji, L-PFR i dalje fiskalizuje lokalno, pamti podatke i šalje ih kasnije kada se veza vrati. Drugim rečima, ne preskačeš L-PFR samo zato što je mreža dostupna.

Ako radiš u V-PFR režimu, onda je tok drugačiji:
UI / kontrolni softver → ESIR → V-PFR u mreži Poreske uprave → ESIR → kupac / štampač. U tom režimu nemaš svoj lokalni PFR u glavnom toku; ESIR ide direktno na državni V-PFR. To je baš taj slučaj koji Poreska uprava opisuje kao izdavanje računa kroz procesor fiskalnih računa “u Sistemu za upravljanje fiskalizacijom, u mreži Poreske uprave”, i zato je taj režim vezan za stalnu internet vezu u realnom vremenu.

Što se tiče trenutka štampe, fizički račun se štampa tek nakon što je fiskalizacija završena i nakon što PFR vrati fiskalizovane podatke ESIR-u. To se vidi iz zvanične definicije PFR-a: on formira račun, pravi potpis i prosleđuje fiskalni račun ESIR-u, a iz tehničkog uputstva se vidi da podatke u zaglavlju računa formira V-PFR ili L-PFR tokom fiskalizacije i vraća ih ESIR-u u sklopu odgovora na zahtev za fiskalizaciju. Tek tada ESIR ima kompletan fiskalni rezultat koji može da isporuči kupcu.

Zato je praktična slika ovakva:
u L-PFR režimu štampaš kada L-PFR vrati fiskalizovan odgovor ESIR-u;
u V-PFR režimu štampaš kada V-PFR vrati fiskalizovan odgovor ESIR-u. U oba slučaja, štampa i dostavljanje računa su na strani ESIR-a, ne na strani PFR-a. To se lepo vidi iz tehničkog uputstva: u okviru ESIR zahteva postoje posebne sekcije za štampanje, za podržane štampače i za dostavljanje fiskalnog računa, pri čemu ESIR može podržavati papirnu ili elektronsku formu isporuke.

Najkraća verzija:

L-PFR režim: ESIR uvek ide preko tvog L-PFR-a; internet samo određuje da li će upload ka Poreskoj ići odmah ili kasnije.
V-PFR režim: ESIR ide direktno na državni V-PFR; zato traži stalnu internet vezu.
Štampa: posle uspešne fiskalizacije, kada ESIR dobije nazad fiskalni odgovor i onda ga isporuči na papir ili elektronski.

Ako hoćeš, sledeće ću ti nacrtati sekvencijski tok za oba režima u tvom stilu, korak po korak, bez državne terminološke magle.

Znaci da bi trebalo da esir na kraju samom izbaci striktno definisan racun, a restoran bi trebalo da preko konfiguracije (ili onaj ko im je napravio aplikaciju) definise sta se dalje sa tim racunom radi - da li ide na stampu ili slicno. Znaci ja i tu mogu da uletim sa adapterima u ponudi?

Tehničko uputstvo je jasno da PFR vraća fiskalni račun ESIR-u, a da ESIR deklariše štampanje i dostavljanje računa.

Prva korekcija:
ne bih govorio da “ESIR sklapa finalni račun pa ga samo prosleđuje”. Bolje je ovako:

ESIR sklapa zahtev za fiskalizaciju / poslovni prikaz računa
PFR od toga pravi fiskalno važeći rezultat
pa ESIR taj rezultat isporučuje dalje.

To je bliže zvaničnoj podeli odgovornosti.

Druga korekcija:
ako podržavaš i V-PFR režim, onda arhitektura nije “ESIR uvek ide na L-PFR”, nego:

ESIR -> LPfrAdapter -> L-PFR
ili
ESIR -> VPfrAdapter -> V-PFR

Poreska uprava eksplicitno razlikuje lokalni PFR i PFR u mreži Poreske uprave.

Znači, najčistiji model je:

Core moduli

ReceiptComposition u ESIR-u
FiscalizationProcessing u PFR-u

Adapteri

SecurityElementAdapter
TaxAuthoritySubmissionAdapter
PrinterAdapter
ElectronicDeliveryAdapter
eventualno JournalStoreAdapter

Važna granica:
štampa je na kraju ESIR-side responsibility, ali tek posle uspešne fiskalizacije i vraćenog odgovora iz PFR-a. Tehničko uputstvo traži da ESIR navede tipove štampe, štampače i tipove dostavljanja računa.
